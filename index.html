The Mission

Scalable community and research infrastructure for independent AI safety research.

A lot of people want to start working on AI safety but don't know where to begin. We're building the public infrastructure to fix that — turning an open-source repository into a structured apprenticeship where every issue is a self-contained experiment you can claim, run, and carry through to publication.

01
The Problem

The onboarding gap

Interest in AI safety is enormous. The number of people who can find a clear, ownable place to start is not.

Motivated newcomers stall in the space between wanting to help and finding a concrete first experiment. There's no shortage of reading lists — but reading lists don't review your code, integrate your results, or carry them to publication.

The Open Safety Project closes that gap with a single, reusable pattern: every GitHub issue is a self-contained experiment. Mentors draft, label, and assign experiments as tickets; junior researchers claim and own them; mentors review, integrate, and help carry the work through to publication.

Coordination runs on GitHub Projects, communication over Slack, with optional Claude Code integration so mentors can log the exact commands behind each result.

For Newcomers

New to AI safety? Start here

In one minute

AI safety is the field working to make powerful AI systems reliable, honest, and controllable — so that as models get more capable, they stay aligned with what people actually want.

Most hands-on technical work clusters into a few practical tracks. This project's templates focus on two of them: evaluations and mechanistic interpretability. You don't need a PhD — you need to be able to read code and run an experiment.

06
For Mentors & Research Leads

Start your own project

Already mentoring — or ready to? This isn't just our pipeline. It's a pattern you can fork to run your own line of research with a cohort behind it.

Most research mentorship doesn't scale. It lives in DMs, scattered docs, and one-off calls, so a senior researcher can carry only a person or two at a time. The bottleneck usually isn't talent or interest — it's the absence of a structure that turns either one into shippable, reviewable work.

The issue-as-experiment model removes that bottleneck. You decompose a research question into self-contained tickets; newcomers claim and run them; you review and integrate. The same machinery that onboards a junior researcher is the machinery that moves your research forward.

Below: why running your project this way beats doing it ad hoc.

Why do it this way
Reason 01

Scale yourself

One mentor, a whole cohort. Ticketed experiments let you support many researchers in parallel instead of one or two ad hoc — your review time is the only thing that has to scale.

Reason 02

Ownable, parallel work

Splitting a research line into self-contained issues lets contributors move at once without colliding — and gives each of them something real, and creditable, to own.

Reason 03

Provenance by default

Every result lands as a pull request with the exact commands behind it — optionally logged via Claude Code. The path from hypothesis to figure stays auditable and reproducible.

Reason 04

A real path to publication

The review → integrate → publish loop is built in, so junior work actually reaches papers. Contributors earn credit; you get throughput toward output that counts.

Reason 05

A front door, not a closed lab

Public-by-default issues turn "I want to help" into a claimable first task. Your repo becomes the on-ramp that recruits and trains the next contributor for you.

Reason 06

Don't build the machinery

Labels, issue templates, the project board, and automations all ship in the template. Fork it once and your pipeline stands up in an afternoon — not a quarter.
